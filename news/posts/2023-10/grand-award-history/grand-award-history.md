The LA County Science & Engineering Fair has been running for more than five decades. Looking back at our grand award winners is like reading a compressed history of science itself — the questions students chose reflect the anxieties, discoveries, and possibilities of each era.

## A Decade-by-Decade Snapshot

### 1970s — The Environment Emerges

The decade of the Clean Air Act and the first Earth Day shaped a generation of student researchers. Early winners studied smog particulates in the San Gabriel Valley, DDT levels in local bird eggs, and oil spill remediation in tidepools. The tools were simple; the questions were urgent.

### 1980s — Computing Arrives

The first projects using personal computers appeared in 1981. By 1988, two of the five grand awards involved computational modeling. Students simulated traffic patterns, modeled epidemic spread, and wrote their first machine-learning precursors — decision trees hand-coded in BASIC.

### 1990s — Biology Goes Molecular

The Human Genome Project cast a long shadow over student labs. PCR became accessible to advanced high schoolers by mid-decade, and projects in genetics, microbiology, and pharmacology multiplied rapidly. Three 1990s LACSEF winners went on to careers directly connected to their fair projects.

### 2000s — The Internet as a Research Tool

Online databases, open-access journals, and early citizen science platforms transformed what students could study. Projects analyzing global datasets, tracking online behavior, and modeling social networks appeared. The gap between what a student could investigate and what a university lab could investigate began to narrow.

### 2010s — Machine Learning and Climate

Two themes dominated: applying neural networks to everything and measuring climate change at the local level. A 2016 project detecting early-stage skin cancer from smartphone images was written up in a dermatology journal the following year.

### 2020s — The Pandemic and Beyond

COVID-19 years produced an extraordinary cohort of epidemiology, public health, and bioinformatics projects. Students tracked misinformation, modeled vaccine hesitancy, and designed low-cost ventilators. The 2023 grand prize — AI-assisted retinopathy screening from consumer hardware — represents where we are now: cheap sensors, powerful models, and students who have grown up with both.

## What Has Not Changed

The questions that win are not the most technically complex. They are the most clearly asked. Across 50 years and hundreds of grand awards, the common thread is a student who could explain, in plain language, exactly what they wanted to know and why it mattered.

That has always been the point.
