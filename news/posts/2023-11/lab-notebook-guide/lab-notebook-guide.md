Your lab notebook is not a rough draft — it is the **contemporaneous record of your original research**. In a professional setting, it is a legal document. At LACSEF, judges may ask to see it, and a well-kept notebook is a credibility signal.

## The Core Rules

1. **Write in pen, never pencil.** Pencil entries can be erased; the notebook's integrity depends on permanence.
2. **Date every entry.** The date shows the sequence of your work and helps establish priority.
3. **Never remove pages or use correction fluid.** Draw a single line through mistakes, initial it, and write the correction next to it.
4. **Write on the day, not the day after.** Memory degrades. If you write an entry 72 hours later, say so explicitly.

## What to Write Every Session

- Date and start/end time
- What you planned to do
- What you actually did (in enough detail that someone else could repeat it)
- Raw data, including failed trials
- Observations that surprised you
- What you plan to do next

## What Judges Look For

Judges are not looking for a perfect notebook — they are looking for an honest one. A notebook with documented failures, revised hypotheses, and dead ends tells them that a real student did this work.

> "A spotless notebook with no mistakes worries me more than a messy one. Science is messy. Show me the messy." — Longtime LACSEF judge

## Digital Notebooks

Some students maintain a digital lab notebook alongside a physical one. This is permitted, but you must print and include digital entries in a bound format. Loose printed pages are not accepted.
